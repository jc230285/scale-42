Hydropower-first: why Norway anchors our pipeline

Long-duration hydropower behaves nothing like wind or solar at the substation. For 24/7 AI workloads, that distinction decides whether a site is investable or marginal.

Will Tasney & Jamie Stewart, CEO & CSO • April 30, 2026

StrategyNorwayEnergy

The phrase “powered by renewables” has been worn smooth by repetition. The version that matters for compute is narrower: the workload runs 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, and the energy sitting behind it has to do the same. Hydropower does. Most other renewables do not.

Compute is a base-load problem

An AI training cluster does not flex. It does not pause for cloud cover or feather its blades on a calm afternoon. It pulls steady, contracted, identical megawatts from the moment it boots until the moment the customer powers it down — typically months later. Anyone telling you otherwise is mistaking the marketing claim for the load profile.

That mismatch — base-load demand against intermittent supply — is the problem the rest of the European grid is now spending billions to solve, with batteries, gas peakers, demand response, and cross-border interconnection. We chose to solve it by going to the places where the supply problem doesn't exist.

What hydropower does that wind and solar don't

A run-of-river or reservoir-fed turbine in Nordland or Trøndelag has three properties that a utility-scale wind farm doesn't:

  • Storage is built in. The reservoir is the battery. The water sitting in it represents weeks or months of full-tilt generation, dispatchable on demand, with round-trip losses near zero. No grid-scale chemistry has caught up to that, and likely won't this decade.
  • The duration curve is flat. Norwegian hydro produces close to nameplate for the high-priced winter months and dispatches around price the rest of the year. That is the inverse of solar's duration curve and the right shape for AI compute.
  • The asset doesn't degrade. A modern wind turbine has a 25-year accounting life. A hydropower plant in Glomfjord has been running since the 1920s and the marginal generation cost has been falling, not rising, the entire time.

Why this is a Norway answer specifically

Iceland has geothermal, which solves the same problem differently — uniform output, no fuel, multi-decade asset life. We like geothermal too, which is why Bakki is in our portfolio. But Norway's hydropower base is roughly 30 GW of installed capacity against a population of 5.5 million. The surplus available for industrial loads, in the locations where transmission is unconstrained, is at a scale and price that no other European country can match.

Sweden and Finland have meaningful hydropower as well, and we develop sites in both. The difference, at this point in the cycle, is that Norway has more stranded capacity in coastal locations — old smelter and ferro-alloy sites whose owners need to either replace the load or re-engineer the grid connection. We arrive offering to take the connection.

What it looks like on the cap table

The price you sign for in year one isn't the price that matters. The price you'll sign for in year ten is.

A 12-year PPA on Nordic hydropower today prices below most spot-market alternatives in the EU. The interesting number, though, is the floor: even on the low end of historical scenarios, Nordic hydro PPAs do not price like a fuel-linked asset. They price like an annuity on a fixed asset whose marginal cost is the operations and maintenance of a turbine. That floor is what makes a 15-year customer commitment underwriteable.

It is also what makes us bid for hydropower-anchored sites first, geothermal second, and onshore wind only when paired with a firm grid contract that covers the gaps. The energy strategy and the capital strategy are the same strategy.

What this rules out

We do not develop sites that depend on grid imports to cover the duration gap. We do not develop sites where the renewable claim is a green certificate purchased on a separate balance sheet from the molecules. And we do not develop sites whose long-term economics rest on the assumption that intermittent prices will keep falling forever — a forecast that has been wrong every time it has been tested.

What's left after those filters is a smaller list than the brochures suggest. It is also the list that will still be operating, profitably, in 2045.
