Free-air cooling at latitude 66°: what works, what doesn't

Glomfjord operations notes — when ambient cooling carries 30 kW racks straight through summer, where direct-liquid takes over, and the density threshold where the trade flips.

James Collins & Roald Normann, CTO & Senior Project Manager • April 30, 2026

OperationsCoolingGlomfjord

Free-air cooling sounds like a free lunch when you read the Glomfjord weather data: 366 days a year below 25 °C, prevailing wind off the fjord, no cooling tower required. The reality is more interesting. Latitude buys you a generous envelope, not a guarantee.

What “free” actually means

“Free-air cooling” is a misnomer in two senses. The fans still draw electricity — typically 5–10% of IT load even in optimal conditions. And the air, while cold, is also wet, salt-laden, and seasonally pollen-rich. Filtering and conditioning that air does not break the economics, but it is not free.

What latitude 66° gives us is a usable thermodynamic gradient nearly every hour of the year. The question is what density of compute that gradient can carry through the worst case — three-day high-pressure systems in July when the fjord goes still and the ambient sits at 22–24 °C.

The summer worst case

We have run instrumented trials on Glomfjord air handlers for three full summer cycles. The findings are unromantic and consistent:

  • Up to ~25 kW per rack: direct evaporative-assisted free air handles the worst-case July week without breaching ASHRAE A1 inlet bounds. PUE in the high 1.0s.
  • 25–35 kW per rack: free air still does most of the work, but a brief mechanical-assist window is needed during the worst 50–80 hours per year. Net annualised PUE around 1.1.
  • Above ~40 kW per rack: free air is no longer the right primary. The economics flip toward direct-liquid at the rack level, with the chiller plant sized only for water rejection rather than air.

That last threshold is where most modern AI training racks now sit. H200-class systems clear 40 kW comfortably. B200 platforms push past 60 kW. The fjord doesn't get any colder; the racks get hotter. The crossover point arrived faster than most operators expected.

The mistake we made early

On the first hall at Glomfjord we sized the air-side plant to handle the published TDP of the customer's planned generation. By the time the hall was live, the customer had refreshed the silicon, the racks were running 1.4× the original spec, and the air handlers were the bottleneck for 90 hours that summer.

We learned to size the cooling plant for the silicon roadmap, not the silicon at deployment day.

That has changed how we design every subsequent hall. We now build the air-side capacity to comfortably carry the customer's day-one density and pre-plumb for liquid cooling on every rack — even the ones that won't initially need it. The marginal cost of doing the plumbing on day one is single-digit thousands per rack. The cost of retrofitting it after the customer has loaded the hall is the customer's downtime, which is a number they will not accept.

Where free air still wins

Latitude is not redundant; it is just no longer the whole story. Three things that latitude 66° still earns us, free of charge:

  • Lower water consumption. Even with evaporative assist on the worst weeks, our annualised water draw is a fraction of comparable mid-latitude sites. That matters more every year as water permitting tightens.
  • Cooler return-water temperatures into the chiller plant, which means smaller heat-exchangers, lower pumping power, and a chiller plant that runs for fewer hours a year — all of which lowers the total cost of the liquid loop, not just the air loop.
  • Wider operating envelope on inlet temperature. Even when the silicon outpaces free-air's primary role, our cold supply is colder than a Frankfurt or Dublin hall could deliver. The customer can run their gear at the bottom of the spec.

What the next hall looks like

Liquid first, air second. The air-side plant exists to cool the rooms the racks are in, not the racks themselves. That's a different size, different airflow strategy, and a much smaller chiller. It also means the next hall in Glomfjord is denser — more useful MW per square metre — and produces useful thermal output in the form of warm water that can feed a district loop. The next site we develop will get that loop on day one.

Latitude is a nice cushion. It is not the design.
